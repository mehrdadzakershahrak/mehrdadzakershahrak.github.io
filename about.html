About

Get to know
Dr. Mehrdad Zaker

Mehrdad Zaker works at the point where AI stops being a concept and starts becoming a system people can actually use. His work sits across foundation models, robotics, decision systems, and secure infrastructure, but the thread running through it is simple: build reasoning systems that are streamlined, trustworthy, and genuinely helpful in the environments where decisions have consequences.

That makes him especially interesting to an audience trying to separate signal from noise in AI. He is not focused on novelty for its own sake, and he is not interested in shipping intelligence as a flashy layer on top of weak foundations. The emphasis is on systems that can reason clearly, fit real workflows, and improve over time. Whether the problem looks like search and recommendations, an agentic workflow, a robotics platform, or a production AI deployment, the question is the same: can this system hold up when it matters?

That perspective has been shaped by both research depth and production experience. As Founding AI Engineer at Neural Intelligence Labs, he has been helping shape the architecture and technical direction of automated reasoning platforms built on foundation models. Before that, at Grainger in Chicago, he led machine learning work on search, ranking, and recommendation systems at scale. Earlier roles involved building the engineering foundations for software platforms meant to last, which helps explain why his approach to AI is usually architectural before it is theatrical.

His academic background adds range without making the story feel scattered. Zaker holds a Ph.D. in Computer Science from Arizona State University, along with master's degrees in Computer Science and Information Technology and a bachelor's degree in Computer Engineering. Across that path, he developed a habit of thinking about intelligence as a whole system: the model, the data, the interface, the feedback loops, the evaluation framework, and the operational constraints all belong in the same conversation.

That systems view is visible in the span of his work. He has worked across LLMs, retrieval-augmented generation, agentic and multi-model AI systems, reinforcement learning, sequential decision-making, experimentation, platform design, model serving, and system health. His research has also moved comfortably between software and physical autonomy, including SLAM-based indoor navigation, ROS self-driving platforms, VR-operated robotic arms with deep-learning grasping, and multi-agent planning environments. There is also a practical inventive streak in the mix, including a U.S. patent application for an autonomous robotic vending machine system and authorship connected to a forthcoming Springer handbook on human-AI interaction.

For people arriving here through his demos, writing, or applied AI work, that is probably the clearest introduction: Mehrdad is a builder who thinks in full loops. He cares about how systems reason, how they fail, how they recover, and how they become useful enough to earn trust. Outside of engineering, martial arts, chess, santoor, and amateur radio seem to reinforce the same sensibility: precision, patience, and respect for systems that only reveal their depth when you stay with them.
