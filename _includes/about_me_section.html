Dr. Mehrdad Zaker

I work at the point where AI stops being a concept and starts becoming a system people can actually use. My work sits across foundation models, robotics, decision systems, and secure infrastructure, but the thread running through it is simple: build reasoning systems that are streamlined, trustworthy, and genuinely helpful in environments where decisions have consequences.

I am most interested in the hard middle between a promising demo and a durable production system. That is where retrieval quality, evaluation, latency, data access, interface design, security review, and operational ownership start to matter. Whether the problem looks like search and recommendations, a RAG pipeline, an agentic workflow, a robotics platform, or a private AI deployment, the question is the same: can this system hold up when it matters?

That perspective has been shaped by research depth and production experience. As Founding AI Engineer at Neural Intelligence Labs, I help shape the architecture and technical direction of automated reasoning platforms built on foundation models. Before that, at Grainger in Chicago, I led machine learning work on search, ranking, and recommendation systems at scale. Earlier roles involved building software foundations for platforms meant to last, which is why my approach to AI is usually architectural before it is theatrical.

My academic background adds range to that systems view. I hold a Ph.D. in Computer Science from Arizona State University, along with master's degrees in Computer Science and Information Technology and a bachelor's degree in Computer Engineering. Across that path, I developed a habit of thinking about intelligence as a whole system: the model, the data, the interface, the feedback loops, the evaluation framework, and the operational constraints all belong in the same conversation.

The work spans LLMs, retrieval-augmented generation, agentic and multi-model AI systems, reinforcement learning, sequential decision-making, experimentation, platform design, model serving, and system health. My research has also moved between software and physical autonomy, including SLAM-based indoor navigation, ROS self-driving platforms, VR-operated robotic arms with deep-learning grasping, and multi-agent planning environments. There is also a practical inventive streak in the mix, including a U.S. patent application for an autonomous robotic vending machine system and authorship connected to a forthcoming Springer handbook on human-AI interaction.

For people arriving here through demos, writing, or applied AI work, the clearest introduction is this: I build in full loops. I care about how systems reason, how they fail, how they recover, and how they become useful enough to earn trust. Outside engineering, martial arts, chess, santoor, and amateur radio reinforce the same sensibility: precision, patience, and respect for systems that only reveal their depth when you stay with them.
